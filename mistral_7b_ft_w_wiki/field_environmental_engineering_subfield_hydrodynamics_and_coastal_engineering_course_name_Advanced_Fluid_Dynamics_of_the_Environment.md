Vorticity is a fundamental concept in fluid dynamics that describes the local spinning motion of a fluid element. It is a vector quantity, defined as the curl of the velocity field, and is denoted by $\vec{\omega}$. In this section, we will explore the vorticity theorems for homogeneous and stratified fluids, which provide a deeper understanding of the behavior of fluid flows.

In a three-dimensional flow, the vorticity vector is defined as the curl of the velocity field $\vec{v}$, given by:

$$
\vec{\omega} = \nabla \times \vec{v}
$$

In a two-dimensional flow where the velocity is independent of the $z$-coordinate and has no $z$-component, the vorticity vector is always parallel to the $z$-axis, and therefore can be expressed as a scalar field multiplied by a constant unit vector $\hat{z}$:

$$
\vec{\omega} = \omega \hat{z}
$$

The vorticity is also related to the flow's circulation (line integral of the velocity) along a closed path by the (classical) Stokes' theorem. Namely, for any infinitesimal surface element with normal direction $\vec{n}$ and area $dA$, the circulation $d\Gamma$ along the perimeter of $C$ is the dot product $\vec{\omega} \cdot (\vec{n} \, dA)$, where $\vec{\omega}$ is the vorticity at the center of $C$.

The evolution of the vorticity field in time is described by the vorticity equation, which can be derived from the Navier–Stokes equations. This equation is particularly useful in understanding the behavior of fluid flows, as it provides a way to track the evolution of vorticity in a fluid.

In many real flows where the viscosity can be neglected (more precisely, in flows with high Reynolds number), the vorticity field can be modeled by a collection of discrete vortices, the vorticity being negligible everywhere except in small regions of space surrounding the axes of the vortices. #### 2.7a Understanding Aerosols

Aerosols are a crucial component of the Earth's atmosphere, playing a significant role in climate, air quality, and human health. They are defined as a suspension system of solid or liquid particles in a gas, with air being the most common suspending gas. The study of aerosols involves understanding their physical properties, sources, and the processes that govern their formation and evolution.

#### 2.7a.1 Physical Properties of Aerosols

Aerosols can be classified based on their physical properties, including size, shape, and density. The size of aerosol particles can range from nanometers to micrometers, and their shape can be spherical, cylindrical, or irregular. The density of aerosol particles can vary significantly, depending on their composition and the gas in which they are suspended.

The size distribution of aerosol particles is a critical parameter in understanding their behavior in the atmosphere. The size distribution can be represented as a function of the number of particles per unit volume as a function of particle size, or as a function of the mass of particles per unit volume as a function of particle size. The size distribution can be determined experimentally by counting the number of particles in different size bins or by measuring the mass of particles in different size bins.

#### 2.7a.2 Sources of Aerosols

Aerosols can be generated from both natural and anthropogenic sources. Natural sources include dust, sea salt, and volcanic eruptions. Anthropogenic sources include emissions from transportation, industry, and energy production. These emissions can contain a variety of gases, liquid droplets, and particulate matter, which can form aerosols in the atmosphere.

#### 2.7a.3 Processes Governing Aerosol Formation and Evolution

The formation and evolution of aerosols are governed by a variety of processes, including nucleation, condensation, coagulation, and sedimentation. Nucleation is the process by which aerosol particles form from a gas or liquid. Condensation is the process by which aerosol particles grow in size as they absorb or react with gases in the atmosphere. Coagulation is the process by which aerosol particles combine to form larger particles. Sedimentation is the process by which aerosol particles settle out of the atmosphere due to gravity.

#### 2.7a.4 Role of Aerosols in Climate

Aerosols play a significant role in climate by influencing the Earth's energy balance. They can directly reflect incoming solar radiation back into space, which can cool the Earth's surface. They can also indirectly affect climate by acting as cloud condensation nuclei, influencing cloud properties and their impact on climate.

#### 2.7a.5 Health Effects of Aerosols

Aerosols can have significant health effects, both directly and indirectly. Direct effects include the inhalation of aerosol particles, which can cause respiratory diseases. Indirect effects include the influence of aerosols on cloud formation and precipitation, which can affect air quality and human health.

#### 2.7a.6 Aerosol Measurement Techniques

Aerosol particles can be measured using a variety of techniques, including optical methods, mass methods, and scanning probe methods. Optical methods, such as light scattering and extinction, can provide information about the size and number concentration of aerosol particles. Mass methods, such as filter sampling and gravimetric analysis, can provide information about the mass concentration of aerosol particles. Scanning probe methods, such as atomic force microscopy, can provide information about the size, shape, and surface properties of aerosol particles.

#### 2.7a.7 Aerosol Modeling

Aerosol modeling involves the use of mathematical models to simulate the formation, evolution, and impact of aerosols in the atmosphere.
