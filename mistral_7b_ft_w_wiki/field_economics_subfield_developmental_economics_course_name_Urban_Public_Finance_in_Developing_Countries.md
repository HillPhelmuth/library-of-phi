This change can be represented as a movement along the production possibility frontier (PPF), with the taxed good becoming more scarce and the non-taxed good becoming more abundant.

The incidence of a tax can be classified into two types: direct incidence and indirect incidence. Direct incidence refers to the immediate effect of a tax on the group that is directly affected by it. For example, if a tax is imposed on the sale of a good, the direct incidence of the tax falls on the seller.

Indirect incidence, on the other hand, refers to the effect of a tax on groups that are not directly affected by it. This can occur through the process of pass-through, where the tax is passed on to the ultimate consumer. For example, if a tax is imposed on the sale of a good, the seller may choose to pass on the tax to the buyer by increasing the price of the good. In this case, the indirect incidence of the tax falls on the buyer.

The concept of tax incidence is closely related to the concept of tax shifting. Tax shifting refers to the ability of a group to pass on the burden of a tax to another group. In the example above, the seller was able to shift the tax burden to the buyer through pass-through.

#### 3.1b General Equilibrium and Public Goods

In the previous sections, we have discussed the concept of general equilibrium and tax incidence. Now, we will explore the role of public goods in the general equilibrium model.

Public goods are non-excludable and non-rivalrous goods. ### Subsection: 5.5a Structural Adjustment and Public Finance

Structural adjustment programs (SAPs) have been a controversial topic in the field of public finance, particularly in developing countries. These programs, often implemented by international lending agencies such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, aim to address economic imbalances and promote long-term growth. However, their impact on public finance has been a subject of debate.

#### 5.5a Structural Adjustment and Public Finance

Structural adjustment programs are typically implemented in response to economic crises, such as high inflation, large current account deficits, or unsustainable levels of public debt. The IMF and World Bank provide loans to these countries, often referred to as structural adjustment loans (SALs), with the condition that the borrowing country implement certain policies. These policies are designed to address the economic imbalances that led to the crisis and to promote long-term growth.

One of the key policies required by SAPs is the reduction of government deficits. This is often achieved through a combination of increased tax revenue and reduced government spending. The rationale behind this policy is that high deficits can lead to inflation, which can have detrimental effects on public services, as discussed in the previous section. By reducing deficits, the government can help control inflation and create a more stable environment for public services.

However, the implementation of these policies can have significant implications for public finance. The increased tax revenue can help to finance public services, but it can also lead to increased government debt. This can be problematic if the government is already struggling with high levels of debt. On the other hand, reduced government spending can help to reduce debt, but it can also lead to a decrease in public services, which can have negative impacts on the population.

Moreover, the conditionality clauses attached to SAPs have been criticized for their effects on the social sector. Critics argue that the financial threats to poor countries amount to blackmail, and that poor nations have no choice but to comply. This can lead to a decrease in government spending on social services, such as education and healthcare, which can have long-term negative impacts on the population.

In conclusion, while structural adjustment programs can help to address economic imbalances and promote long-term growth, their impact on public finance is complex and requires careful consideration. The implementation of these programs should be accompanied by a comprehensive understanding of their potential effects on public services and the population as a whole.

### Section: 6.1 MicroFinance History and Design:

#### 6.1a MicroFinance and Public Finance

Microfinance, a concept that has gained significant attention in recent years, is a financial service provided to low-income individuals, often in developing countries, who lack access to traditional banking services. This service is designed to meet the financial needs of these individuals, who are typically excluded from the formal financial system. Microfinance can take various forms, including microcredit, microinsurance, and micro-savings, among others.

The history of microfinance can be traced back to the 1970s, when Bangladeshi economist Muhammad Yunus founded the Grameen Bank. Yunus's work, which was initially met with skepticism, has since been recognized with numerous awards, including the Nobel Peace Prize in 2006. His work has inspired a global movement that has reached millions of people, particularly women, with access to financial services.

Microfinance has been a topic of interest in the field of public finance due to its potential to address issues of poverty and economic development. The provision of microfinance services can help to increase the incomes of low-income individuals, thereby improving their living standards. Furthermore, microfinance can contribute to the development of local businesses, which can have a positive impact on the local economy.

However, the implementation of microfinance services in developing countries is not without challenges. One of the main challenges is the issue of affordability. Many low-income individuals may not be able to afford the fees associated with microfinance services, particularly if these fees are not subsidized. This can limit the reach of microfinance and prevent it from reaching those who need it most.

Another challenge is the issue of equity. While microfinance has the potential to reach the poorest members of society, there is a risk that it may not always do so. This is particularly true in the context of microcredit, where the wealthiest individuals may be more likely to access credit due to their ability to provide collateral.

#### 6.1b MicroFinance Design and Implementation
