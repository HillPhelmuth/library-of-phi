It was developed in response to the limitations of the Solow model, which did not adequately explain the long-term growth of economies. ### Subsection: 3.3a Understanding Capital Asset Pricing

Capital asset pricing is a fundamental concept in finance that helps investors determine the expected return of an asset based on its risk. It is a crucial tool for investors and portfolio managers, as it allows them to make informed decisions about which assets to include in their portfolios.

#### The Capital Asset Pricing Model

The capital asset pricing model (CAPM) is a widely used model for determining the expected return of an asset. It is based on the idea that the expected return of an asset is equal to the risk-free rate plus a risk premium, which is determined by the asset's beta (a measure of its sensitivity to market movements).

The CAPM formula is as follows:

$$
E(R_i) = R_f + \beta_i(E(R_m) - R_f)
$$

where:
- $E(R_i)$ is the expected return of asset i
- $R_f$ is the risk-free rate
- $\beta_i$ is the beta of asset i
- $E(R_m)$ is the expected return of the market portfolio

#### The Security Market Line

The security market line (SML) is a graphical representation of the CAPM formula. It plots the expected return of an asset against its beta, with the risk-free rate and the expected return of the market portfolio as the intercept and slope, respectively.

The SML is a useful tool for determining if an asset is undervalued or overvalued. If an asset's expected return versus risk is plotted above the SML, it is undervalued since the investor can expect a greater return for the inherent risk. Conversely, if an asset is plotted below the SML, it is overvalued since the investor would be accepting less return for the amount of risk assumed.

#### The Capital Asset Pricing Model and the Market Portfolio

The CAPM assumes that all investors hold the market portfolio, which is a portfolio that includes all risky assets in the market. This assumption is crucial for the model's validity, as it allows for the determination of the expected return of the market portfolio.

However, in reality, not all investors hold the market portfolio. Some investors may choose to hold a portion of their portfolio in risk-free assets, while others may choose to hold a portfolio that is not perfectly correlated with the market portfolio. In these cases, the CAPM may not accurately predict the expected return of an asset.

### Subsection: 3.3b Role of Idiosyncratic and Aggregate Risk

In the previous section, we discussed the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) and its assumptions. However, in reality, not all investors hold the market portfolio, and not all assets are perfectly correlated with the market portfolio. This leads to the concept of idiosyncratic and aggregate risk.

#### Idiosyncratic Risk

Idiosyncratic risk refers to the risk associated with a specific asset or investment. This risk is unique to the asset and is not shared by other assets. In the CAPM, idiosyncratic risk is assumed to be diversifiable, meaning that it can be reduced by holding a diversified portfolio. This assumption is based on the law of large numbers, which states that as the number of assets in a portfolio increases, the impact of any individual asset's return on the portfolio's overall return decreases.

#### Aggregate Risk

Aggregate risk, on the other hand, refers to the risk associated with the market as a whole. This risk is not unique to any particular asset and is shared by all assets. In the CAPM, aggregate risk is assumed to be non-diversifiable, meaning that it cannot be reduced by holding a diversified portfolio. This assumption is based on the idea that all assets are affected by the same macroeconomic factors, such as changes in interest rates or economic growth.

#### The Role of Idiosyncratic and Aggregate Risk in Capital Asset Pricing

The role of idiosyncratic and aggregate risk in capital asset pricing is crucial. Idiosyncratic risk is diversifiable and can be reduced by holding a diversified portfolio. This means that investors can reduce their exposure to idiosyncratic risk by holding a portfolio of assets. However, aggregate risk is non-diversifiable and cannot be reduced by holding a diversified portfolio. This means that investors must accept the market risk associated with aggregate risk.

The Capital Asset Pricing Model takes into account both idiosyncratic and aggregate risk. The expected return of an asset is determined by its beta, which is a measure of its sensitivity to market movements. Beta is calculated by dividing the covariance of an asset's return with the market portfolio's return by the variance of the market portfolio's return. Assets with a beta of 1 are perfectly correlated with the market portfolio and therefore have no idiosyncratic risk. Assets with a beta of less than 1 are less correlated with the market portfolio and therefore have more idiosyncratic risk.

In conclusion, understanding the role of idiosyncratic and aggregate risk is crucial for investors and portfolio managers. By diversifying their portfolios, investors can reduce their exposure to idiosyncratic risk, but they must accept the market risk associated with aggregate risk. The Capital Asset Pricing Model provides a framework for understanding and pricing these risks.

The intensive margin refers to the changes in the amount of labor supplied by an individual, given their existing job. The interaction between the intensive and extensive margins is crucial for understanding the overall labor supply in a village or a family. ### Subsection: 4.2a Understanding Risk Sharing

Risk sharing is a critical aspect of economic decision-making, particularly in the context of labor supply. It refers to the ability of individuals or groups to share the risks associated with their economic decisions. In the context of labor supply, risk sharing can take many forms, from informal arrangements within families to more formal contracts between workers and employers.

One of the key barriers to risk sharing is the issue of moral hazard. Moral hazard refers to the tendency of individuals to change their behavior in response to changes in their risk exposure. For example, if a worker is covered by a comprehensive health insurance policy, they may be more likely to engage in risky behavior, knowing that their medical expenses will be covered. This is an example of moral hazard, as the worker's behavior has changed in response to the change in their risk exposure.

Moral hazard can pose a significant barrier to risk sharing. If individuals are aware of the potential for moral hazard, they may be less willing to share risks, fearing that others will take advantage of the arrangement. This can lead to a lack of risk sharing, which can have significant implications for economic decision-making.

Another barrier to risk sharing is the issue of adverse selection. Adverse selection refers to the tendency of individuals to sort themselves into different groups based on their risk characteristics. For example, if a group of workers is offered a health insurance policy, those with higher health risks may be more likely to join the group, as they will benefit more from the policy. This can lead to an adverse selection problem, as the group may end up with a higher average health risk than was initially expected.

Adverse selection can also pose a significant barrier to risk sharing. #### 4.2b.1 Moral Hazard

Moral hazard is a significant barrier to risk sharing. It occurs when individuals change their behavior in response to changes in their risk exposure. For instance, if a worker is covered by a comprehensive health insurance policy, they may be more likely to engage in risky behavior, knowing that their medical expenses will be covered. This can lead to a lack of risk sharing, as individuals may be less willing to share risks if they perceive that others may take advantage of the arrangement.

To identify moral hazard, we can look at the behavior of individuals before and after a change in their risk exposure. If there is a significant change in behavior, it may be an indication of moral hazard. For example, if a worker's behavior changes significantly after they are covered by a health insurance policy, it may be a sign of moral hazard.

#### 4.2b.2 Adverse Selection

Adverse selection is another barrier to risk sharing. It occurs when individuals sort themselves into different groups based on their risk characteristics. For example, if a group of workers is offered a health insurance policy, those with higher health risks may be more likely to join the group, as they will benefit more from the policy. This can lead to an adverse selection problem, as the group may end up with a higher average health risk than was initially expected.

To identify adverse selection, we can look at the composition of groups before and after a change in risk exposure. If there is a significant change in the composition of a group, it may be an indication of adverse selection. For example, if a group of workers joins a health insurance policy, and the group's average health risk increases significantly, it may be a sign of adverse selection.

#### 4.2b.3 Limited Behavior

Limited behavior is a barrier to risk sharing that occurs when individuals choose easier, yet less effective, pro-environmental behavioral changes. For instance, recycling and using metal straws are easier changes to make, but they may not have a significant impact on the environment. This can lead to a lack of risk sharing, as individuals may be less willing to share risks if they perceive that their efforts will not have a significant impact.

To identify limited behavior, we can look at the behavior of individuals before and after a change in their risk exposure. If there is a significant change in behavior, but it is not aligned with the potential impact of the behavior, it may be an indication of limited behavior. #### 4.2b.4 Switching Costs

Switching costs or switching barriers are terms used in microeconomics, strategic management, and marketing. To identify switching costs, we can look at the behavior of individuals before and after a change in their risk exposure. If there is a significant change in behavior, but it is not aligned with the potential benefits of the new arrangement, it may be an indication of switching costs. ### Subsection: 4.4a Understanding Reduced-form Evaluation

Reduced-form evaluation is a method used to assess the impact of a policy or program by focusing on its direct effects. This approach is often used in the context of development economics, where the goal is to understand the immediate effects of a policy or program on a specific outcome. Reduced-form evaluation is particularly useful when the underlying mechanisms through which a policy or program operates are not fully understood or are complex.

#### 4.4a.1 Concept of Reduced-form Evaluation

Reduced-form evaluation is based on the idea that the outcome of interest can be explained by a set of explanatory variables. These variables can be either exogenous (not influenced by the policy or program) or endogenous (influenced by the policy or program). The goal of reduced-form evaluation is to estimate the effect of the policy or program on the outcome by controlling for the endogenous variables.

#### 4.4a.2 Implementing Reduced-form Evaluation

Implementing reduced-form evaluation involves several steps. First, the outcome of interest and the explanatory variables are identified. Second, a model is specified that relates the outcome to the explanatory variables. This model is often a regression model, but it can also be a structural equation model or a system of equations. Third, the model is estimated using data collected before and after the implementation of the policy or program. Finally, the effect of the policy or program is estimated by comparing the predicted outcome with the actual outcome.

#### 4.4a.3 Advantages and Limitations of Reduced-form Evaluation

Reduced-form evaluation has several advantages. It is relatively easy to implement, especially when the underlying mechanisms through which a policy or program operates are not fully understood or are complex. It also allows for the estimation of the effect of a policy or program on a specific outcome, which can be useful for decision-making. However, reduced-form evaluation also has limitations. It assumes that the model is correctly specified and that the assumptions underlying the model are valid. It also does not account for the potential effects of unobserved variables, which can lead to biased estimates.

### Subsection: 4.4b Role of Structural Evaluation

Structural evaluation is a method used to assess the impact of a policy or program by focusing on its underlying mechanisms. This approach is often used in the context of development economics, where the goal is to understand the long-term effects of a policy or program on a specific outcome. Structural evaluation is particularly useful when the direct effects of a policy or program are not fully understood or are complex.

#### 4.4b.1 Concept of Structural Evaluation

Structural evaluation is based on the idea that the outcome of interest can be explained by a set of underlying mechanisms. These mechanisms can be either exogenous (not influenced by the policy or program) or endogenous (influenced by the policy or program). The goal of structural evaluation is to estimate the effect of the policy or program on the outcome by understanding and modeling these underlying mechanisms.

#### 4.4b.2 Implementing Structural Evaluation

Implementing structural evaluation involves several steps. First, the outcome of interest and the underlying mechanisms are identified. Second, a model is specified that relates the outcome to the underlying mechanisms. This model can be a reduced-form model, a structural model, or a combination of both. Third, the model is estimated using data collected before and after the implementation of the policy or program. Finally, the effect of the policy or program is estimated by comparing the predicted outcome with the actual outcome.

#### 4.4b.3 Advantages and Limitations of Structural Evaluation

Structural evaluation has several advantages. It allows for a more comprehensive understanding of the impact of a policy or program by considering its underlying mechanisms. It also provides insights into the long-term effects of a policy or program, which can be useful for policy design and implementation. However, structural evaluation can be more complex and time-consuming than reduced-form evaluation, and it requires a good understanding of the underlying mechanisms.
