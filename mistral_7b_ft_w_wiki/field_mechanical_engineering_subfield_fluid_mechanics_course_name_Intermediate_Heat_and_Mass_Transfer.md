#### 4.1a Introduction to Turbulence

Turbulence is a complex phenomenon that is present in many natural and industrial processes. It is characterized by chaotic and unpredictable fluctuations in velocity, pressure, and other physical quantities. Understanding and predicting turbulence is crucial for many engineering applications, such as the design of aircraft, ships, and power plants. In this section, we will delve into the world of turbulence, exploring its fundamental principles and applications.

Turbulence is often characterized by the Reynolds number, a dimensionless quantity that is used to classify the degree of turbulence in a flow. The Reynolds number is defined as:

$$
Re = \frac{\rho u L}{\mu}
$$

where $\rho$ is the fluid density, $u$ is the characteristic velocity, $L$ is the characteristic length, and $\mu$ is the dynamic viscosity. A high Reynolds number indicates a turbulent flow, while a low Reynolds number indicates a laminar flow.

The Navier-Stokes equations, the fundamental equations of fluid dynamics, are used to describe turbulent flows. However, these equations are nonlinear and difficult to solve analytically for turbulent flows. Therefore, engineers often use numerical methods, such as the finite difference method, to solve these equations for turbulent flows.

Turbulence modeling is a crucial aspect of engineering and physics. It involves the use of mathematical models to predict the behavior of turbulence in various situations. These models are based on various assumptions and simplifications, and their accuracy depends on the specific flow conditions. Some of the commonly used turbulence models include the Spalart–Allmaras model, the "k"–ε model, and the "k"–ω model.

The Spalart–Allmaras model, for example, uses the Boussinesq hypothesis to model turbulence viscosity transport. The model uses only one additional equation to model turbulence viscosity transport, making it relatively low cost in terms of computational resources. The model is given by:

$$
\frac{\partial \tilde{\nu}}{\partial t} + u_j \frac{\partial \tilde{\nu}}{\partial x_j} = C_{b1} [1 - f_{t2}] \tilde{S} \tilde{\nu} + \frac{1}{\sigma} \{ \nabla \cdot [(\nu + \tilde{\nu}) \nabla \tilde{\nu}] + C_{b2} | \nabla \tilde{\nu} |^2 \} - \left[C_{w1} f_w - \frac{C_{b1}}{\kappa^2} f_{t2}\right] \left( \frac{\tilde{\nu}}{d} \right)^2 + f_{t1} \Delta U^2
$$

where $\tilde{\nu}$ is the turbulence viscosity, $u_j$ is the velocity in the $j$ direction, $f_{t1}$ and $f_{t2}$ are functions of the turbulence viscosity and the distance from the closest surface, and $\Delta U^2$ is the norm of the difference between the velocity at the trip and that at the field point.

#### 5.1c Diffusion

$$
J = -D \frac{dC}{dx}
$$

$$
\frac{dC}{dt} = D \frac{d^2C}{dx^2}
$$
