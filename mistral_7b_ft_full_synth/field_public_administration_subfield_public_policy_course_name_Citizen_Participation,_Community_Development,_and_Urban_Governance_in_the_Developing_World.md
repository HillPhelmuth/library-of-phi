#### 1.3b Evolution of Community Development

The evolution of community development in the developing world has been shaped by a variety of factors, including the changing nature of urban governance, the impact of colonial legacies, and the influence of international development agencies.

##### Early Community Development

The concept of community development emerged in the mid-20th century as a response to the social and economic challenges faced by rural and urban communities in the developing world. Initially, community development was primarily focused on addressing the immediate needs of communities, such as improving access to basic services and infrastructure. This approach was often driven by the belief that by improving the material conditions of communities, their social and economic well-being could be enhanced.

##### Critiques of Early Community Development

However, as the community development approach was implemented in practice, it faced a number of criticisms. One of the main criticisms was that it was too focused on the provision of services and infrastructure, and did not adequately address the underlying social and economic inequalities that were driving community problems. This led to the development of a more participatory approach to community development, which emphasized the importance of community participation and empowerment in the development process.

##### Participatory Community Development

The participatory approach to community development emerged in the 1970s and 1980s, and was heavily influenced by the work of Brazilian educator Paulo Freire. Freire argued that true development could only be achieved through the active participation of community members, who needed to be viewed as subjects rather than objects of development. This approach also emphasized the importance of addressing the root causes of community problems, rather than just providing short-term solutions.

##### Current Trends in Community Development

Today, community development continues to evolve in response to the changing needs and challenges faced by communities in the developing world. There is a growing emphasis on community-driven development, where communities are seen as the primary agents of their own development. This approach recognizes the importance of community ownership and sustainability in the development process.

In addition, there is a growing recognition of the importance of community-based disaster risk management, given the increasing frequency and severity of natural disasters in the developing world. This approach emphasizes the need for communities to be actively involved in disaster risk reduction and preparedness, in order to mitigate the impacts of disasters.

Finally, there is a growing focus on the role of community development in promoting social inclusion and equity. This includes efforts to address the specific needs and challenges faced by marginalized and vulnerable groups, such as women, children, and indigenous communities.

In conclusion, the evolution of community development in the developing world has been shaped by a variety of factors, including the changing nature of urban governance, the impact of colonial legacies, and the influence of international development agencies. As we move forward, it is important to continue to adapt and evolve our approaches to community development in response to the changing needs and challenges faced by communities in the developing world.

#### 2.3a Definition of Deliberation and Public Sphere

Deliberation is a process of collective decision-making where individuals engage in a reasoned exchange of ideas and perspectives, with the aim of reaching a mutually acceptable solution. It is a key component of democratic governance, as it allows for the expression of diverse viewpoints and the consideration of alternative solutions. Deliberation is often facilitated by the public sphere, a space where individuals can come together to freely discuss and identify societal problems, and through that discussion influence political action.

The public sphere, as defined by Jürgen Habermas, is a "society engaged in critical public debate". It is a realm of our social life where individuals can come together to freely discuss and identify societal problems, and through that discussion influence political action. The public sphere is opposed to the private sphere, which is characterized by exclusion and lack of access. The public sphere is intrinsically connected to the private sphere, as it is the public sphere that allows for the expression of private concerns and the articulation of the needs of society with the state.

Habermas defines the conditions of the public sphere as follows:

1. Publicity: The public sphere is open to all citizens, and its proceedings are transparent and accessible to all.
2. Equality: All participants in the public sphere are equal, and their contributions are valued equally.
3. Discursivity: The public sphere is a space of reasoned discussion and debate, where ideas and perspectives are expressed and critically examined.
4. Participation: All citizens have the right and opportunity to participate in the public sphere.
5. Association: Citizens are free to form associations and groups to pursue their interests and advocate for their causes.

The public sphere is not a physical space, but a discursive space where individuals can engage in public debate. It is facilitated by various media, including traditional media such as newspapers and television, as well as new media such as social media and online forums. The public sphere is a crucial component of democratic governance, as it allows for the expression of diverse viewpoints and the consideration of alternative solutions. It is also a space where citizens can hold their governments accountable and influence political action.

#### 4.1a Definition of Participatory Planning and Design

Participatory planning and design is a process that involves the active participation of all stakeholders, including end-users, in the planning and design of a project or program. This approach is based on the principle that those who will be affected by a decision have a right to be involved in the decision-making process. Participatory planning and design is a key tool in community development and urban governance, as it ensures that the needs and aspirations of the community are taken into account in the planning and implementation of projects.

Participatory planning and design is not a new concept. It has its roots in the Scandinavian participatory design movement, which emphasized the importance of involving end-users in the design process. However, the term "co-design" has gained popularity in recent years, particularly in the context of "co-creation/co-design". This terminology shift has led to some confusion, as Elizabeth Sanders and Peter Stappers have pointed out. However, for the purposes of this book, we will continue to use the term "participatory planning and design" to refer to the process of involving end-users in the planning and design of projects.

Participatory planning and design is a complex process that requires a deep understanding of the community and its needs. It involves a range of tools and methods, including community mapping, focus group discussions, and participatory rural appraisal. These tools and methods are used to gather information about the community, its resources, and its needs. This information is then used to develop a plan or design that reflects the aspirations and needs of the community.

Participatory planning and design is not without its challenges. It requires a commitment to involving all stakeholders, which can be difficult in complex and diverse communities. It also requires a willingness to listen and learn from the community, which can be challenging for those who are used to making decisions without input from those who will be affected by them. However, the benefits of participatory planning and design, including increased community ownership and sustainability, make it a valuable tool in community development and urban governance.

#### 4.1b Principles of Participatory Planning and Design

Participatory planning and design is guided by several key principles that ensure the process is inclusive, transparent, and effective. These principles include:

1. **Inclusivity**: All stakeholders, including end-users, should be included in the planning and design process. This ensures that the needs and aspirations of the community are taken into account.

2. **Transparency**: The planning and design process should be transparent, with all decisions and processes openly communicated to all stakeholders. This promotes trust and accountability.

3. **Collaboration**: The planning and design process should be collaborative, with all stakeholders working together towards a common goal. This promotes a sense of ownership and commitment to the project.

4. **Empowerment**: The planning and design process should aim to empower the community, by involving them in decision-making and providing them with the skills and resources to implement the plan.

5. **Sustainability**: The planning and design process should aim to achieve long-term sustainability, by considering the environmental, social, and economic impacts of the project.

These principles are not always easy to achieve, particularly in complex and diverse communities. However, they provide a framework for a participatory planning and design process that is inclusive, transparent, and effective.

#### 4.4a Definition of Collaborative Governance

Collaborative governance is a form of decision-making process that involves the active participation of all stakeholders, including non-state actors, in the public policy or public management process. It is a governing arrangement where one or more public agencies directly engage non-state stakeholders in a collective decision-making process that is formal, consensus-oriented, and deliberative. The aim is to make or implement public policy or manage public programs or assets.

The concept of collaborative governance is rooted in the principles of democracy, transparency, and accountability. It is a response to the growing complexity of public issues and the need for more inclusive and effective decision-making processes. Collaborative governance is not limited to state-initiated arrangements and engagement between government and non-government sectors. It can also involve collaboration between governments at different levels and hybrid partnerships initiated by the private or community sectors.

Collaborative governance is guided by six criteria:

1. The forum is initiated by public agencies.
2. Participants in the forum include non-state actors.
3. Participants engage in decision making and are not merely "consulted".
4. The forum is formally organized.
5. The forum aims to make decisions by consensus.
6. The focus of collaboration is on public policy or public management.

The advantages of effective collaborative governance are numerous. It enables a better and shared understanding of complex problems involving many stakeholders and allows these stakeholders to work together and agree on solutions. It can help policy makers identify and address the root causes of public issues, leading to more effective and sustainable solutions. Collaborative governance also promotes accountability and transparency, as all stakeholders are involved in the decision-making process. This can help build public trust and support for public policies and programs.

### Subsection: 6.2b Impact Assessment Methods for Community Development

Impact assessment is a crucial step in evaluating the effectiveness of community development projects. It allows us to understand the impact of these projects and identify areas for improvement. In this subsection, we will explore the various methods used for impact assessment in community development projects.

#### 6.2b.1 Rigorous Impact Evaluation

Rigorous impact evaluation is a method used to measure the net change in outcomes for a specific group of people that can be attributed to a specific program. This method is used by the International Initiative for Impact Evaluation (3ie) and involves following the best methodology available, feasible, and appropriate to the evaluation question and context. It involves comparing the outcomes of a program against a counterfactual that shows what would have happened to beneficiaries without the program. This method is particularly useful for evaluating the impact of community development projects, as it allows for a more accurate assessment of the project's effectiveness.

#### 6.2b.2 Quasi-Experimental Designs

Quasi-experimental designs are another method used for impact assessment in community development projects. These designs involve comparing the outcomes of a program with a control group that does not receive the program. This method is useful when a randomized controlled trial is not feasible or ethical. However, it may not provide as accurate results as a randomized controlled trial, as there may be differences between the treatment and control groups that cannot be accounted for.

#### 6.2b.3 Case Studies

Case studies are a qualitative method used for impact assessment in community development projects. They involve in-depth analysis of a specific project or program to understand its impact on the community. Case studies can provide valuable insights into the project's effectiveness and identify areas for improvement. However, they may not be as comprehensive as other methods, as they may not capture the impact of the project on the entire community.

#### 6.2b.4 Surveys and Interviews

Surveys and interviews are quantitative methods used for impact assessment in community development projects. Surveys involve collecting data from a sample of individuals to understand their perceptions and experiences with the project. Interviews, on the other hand, involve in-depth discussions with individuals to gain a deeper understanding of their experiences. These methods can provide valuable insights into the project's impact, but they may not capture the full impact of the project on the community.

#### 6.2b.5 Geographic Information Systems (GIS)

Geographic Information Systems (GIS) are a powerful tool for impact assessment in community development projects. They involve using spatial data and analysis to understand the impact of a project on a specific geographic area. GIS can provide a comprehensive understanding of the project's impact and identify areas for improvement. However, it may not be feasible for all projects, as it requires access to spatial data and specialized software.

In conclusion, impact assessment is a crucial step in evaluating the effectiveness of community development projects. By using a combination of methods, we can gain a comprehensive understanding of the project's impact and identify areas for improvement. It is essential for decision-makers and project implementers to use these methods to ensure the success of community development projects.

#### 6.4a.1 Theoretical Framework for Best Practices

Eugene Bardach provides a theoretical framework for understanding best practices in his book "The Implementation Game: How Games Are Played and Won in the Policy Process" (2011). He outlines an eightfold path for best practices, which includes:

1. Identifying the problem: This involves clearly defining the issue or challenge that needs to be addressed.
2. Setting goals and objectives: This involves establishing specific and measurable goals and objectives for addressing the problem.
3. Selecting strategies: This involves choosing the most appropriate strategies for achieving the goals and objectives.
4. Implementing the strategies: This involves putting the selected strategies into action.
5. Monitoring and evaluating progress: This involves regularly assessing the effectiveness of the strategies and making necessary adjustments.
6. Learning from success and failure: This involves studying successful examples and learning from failures to continuously improve practices.
7. Communicating and collaborating: This involves effective communication and collaboration among all stakeholders involved in the process.
8. Building capacity and sustainability: This involves building the capacity of individuals and organizations to sustain the best practices over time.

#### 6.4a.2 Methodology for Best Practices Research

Bretschneider et al. (2005) offer an alternate methodology for Best Practices research. Their approach is more technical and explores issues of completeness and comparability. They address the fact that fully establishing whether a practice is truly a best practice would require assessment in all contexts, while in practice, only example cases are analyzed. Bretschneider also stresses the fact that in order for something to be considered a "best practice" it must be arrived at through a comparative process between methodologies. In order for a "best practice" to be valid, it must be generalizable, replicable, and scalable.
