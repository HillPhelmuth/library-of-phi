This increase in the after-tax rate of return to capital in the non-corporate sector is the incidence of the tax.

In conclusion, the General Equilibrium Model provides a powerful framework for understanding the incidence of taxes. ### Subsection: 5.2a Conditionalities and Public Finance

Conditionalities play a crucial role in the provision of international lending to developing countries. They are a set of policies or conditions that the lending agency requires in exchange for financial resources. These conditions are often associated with economic theory and serve as an enforcement mechanism for repayment.

#### The Concept of Conditionality

Conditionality is a concept that was introduced by the International Monetary Fund (IMF) in the 1950s. It refers to the set of policies or conditions that the IMF requires in exchange for financial resources. These conditions are often associated with economic theory and serve as an enforcement mechanism for repayment.

The theoretical underpinning of conditionality is the "monetary approach to the balance of payments," which was developed by Jacques Polak. This approach emphasizes the role of money supply and demand in determining a country's balance of payments. According to this approach, a country's balance of payments is determined by the interaction of its money supply and demand, and not by its level of economic development.

#### Structural Adjustment and Conditionality

One of the most well-known examples of conditionality is the structural adjustment program. This program is a set of policies or conditions that the IMF requires in exchange for financial resources. These conditions are often associated with the Washington Consensus, which is a set of economic policies that were promoted by the IMF and World Bank in the 1980s and 1990s.

The conditions for structural adjustment can include:

- Reducing government spending
- Privatizing state-owned enterprises
- Liberalizing trade and investment
- Deregulating markets
- Improving the business environment

These conditions are often controversial, as they can have significant impacts on a country's economy and society. Critics argue that these conditions can exacerbate economic inequality and hinder long-term development.

#### Benefits of Conditionality

Despite the controversy surrounding conditionality, there are also benefits to be gained from these policies. By requiring countries to adopt certain policies and reforms, the IMF ensures that the funds lent to them will be used for the purposes defined by the Articles of Agreement. This provides safeguards that the country will be able to rectify its macroeconomic and structural imbalances.

Moreover, conditionality also reassures the IMF that the funds lent to them will be repaid. By requiring countries to adopt certain policies and reforms, the IMF mitigates the incentive problem of moral hazard, where economic agents may maximize their own utility to the detriment of others because they do not bear the full consequences of their actions.

In conclusion, conditionality plays a crucial role in the provision of international lending to developing countries. While it can be controversial, it also serves as an important mechanism for ensuring the repayment of loans and promoting long-term economic development.

### Subsection: 5.5a Structural Adjustment and Public Finance

Structural adjustment programs (SAPs) have been a controversial topic in the field of public finance, particularly in developing countries. These programs, often implemented by international lending agencies such as the International Monetary Fund (IMF) and the World Bank, aim to address economic imbalances and promote long-term growth. However, their effectiveness and potential drawbacks have been heavily debated.

#### The Purpose of Structural Adjustment

The primary purpose of structural adjustment programs is to address economic imbalances and promote long-term growth. These programs are typically implemented in response to economic crises, such as high inflation, large current account deficits, or unsustainable levels of public debt. The IMF and World Bank require borrowing countries to implement certain policies in order to obtain new loans or to lower interest rates on existing ones. These policies are typically centered around increased privatization, liberalizing trade and foreign investment, and balancing government deficit.

#### The Role of Structural Adjustment in Public Finance

Structural adjustment programs can have significant implications for public finance in developing countries. On one hand, these programs can help to address fiscal imbalances and promote long-term growth. By implementing policies that increase government revenue and reduce expenditure, structural adjustment can help to balance the government's budget and reduce inflation. This can have positive effects on public services, such as education and healthcare, by increasing funding and improving the quality of these services.

On the other hand, structural adjustment can also have negative effects on public finance. The conditionality clauses attached to loans can be criticized for their potential to exacerbate social inequalities. For example, the privatization of public services can lead to increased costs for the poor, who may not be able to afford these services. Moreover, the implementation of free market policies can lead to a decrease in government revenue, particularly in countries that rely heavily on natural resources. This can result in a decrease in funding for public services, which can have detrimental effects on the population.

#### The Future of Structural Adjustment

In recent years, there has been a shift in the approach to structural adjustment. Some proponents of structural adjustment, such as the World Bank, have begun to focus on "poverty reduction" as a goal. This shift recognizes the potential negative effects of structural adjustment on the poor and aims to mitigate these effects. Additionally, there has been a push to increase the involvement of the borrowing country in the design and implementation of structural adjustment programs. This is meant to address criticisms that these programs are too generic and do not take into account the specific needs and circumstances of each country.

In conclusion, structural adjustment programs have been a contentious topic in the field of public finance. While they can help to address economic imbalances and promote long-term growth, they also have the potential to exacerbate social inequalities. As such, it is important for policymakers to carefully consider the potential effects of these programs and to work towards a more inclusive and sustainable approach to structural adjustment.

This theory has been influential in shaping the policies of many developing countries, and has led to a shift away from traditional structural adjustment programs towards more market-oriented policies.

#### 6.1a MicroFinance and Public Finance

Microfinance is a form of public finance that focuses on providing financial services to the poor. It is designed to address the financial constraints faced by the poor, who often lack access to formal financial services due to factors such as low income, lack of collateral, and high transaction costs. Microfinance aims to provide the poor with access to financial services, such as loans, savings, and insurance, that can help them manage their finances and improve their living conditions.

The history of microfinance can be traced back to the 1970s, when a group of economists and social scientists began exploring the concept of microcredit as a means to address poverty. The idea was to provide small loans to the poor, who were often excluded from the formal financial system, to help them start or expand their businesses. This approach was based on the belief that the poor, like any other group, have the ability and desire to improve their lives, but often lack the necessary resources and opportunities.

Over the years, microfinance has evolved and expanded to include a range of financial services, beyond just loans. These include savings, insurance, and remittance services. Microfinance institutions (MFIs) have also evolved to become more sophisticated and professional, with many now operating as commercial entities. This has allowed them to access capital markets and attract private investors, thereby reducing their reliance on donor funding.

The design of microfinance is based on the principles of sustainability and scalability. Sustainability refers to the ability of microfinance to continue operating in the long term, without relying on donor funding. This is achieved through a combination of commercial lending and subsidies, where the latter is gradually reduced as the institution becomes more financially sustainable. Scalability, on the other hand, refers to the ability of microfinance to reach a large number of clients. This is achieved through a focus on efficiency and cost-effectiveness, as well as the use of technology and innovation.

Microfinance has been successful in reaching a large number of clients in developing countries. According to the Microcredit Summit Campaign, as of 2015, over 100 million poor people had access to microfinance services, with a repayment rate of over 98%. However, there are still many challenges to overcome, particularly in terms of reaching the poorest of the poor and ensuring that microfinance services are affordable and sustainable.

#### 6.1b MicroFinance Design and Implementation

The implementation process involves setting up the necessary infrastructure, recruiting and training staff, and launching the program.

##### Design

The design of a microfinance program begins with identifying the target population. This is typically the poor, who are often excluded from the formal financial system due to factors such as low income, lack of collateral, and high transaction costs. The target population can be further segmented based on factors such as location, occupation, and social status.

The next step is to determine the type of financial services to be provided. This can include loans, savings, and insurance. Loans are the most common type of service, as they can help the poor start or expand their businesses. Savings services can help the poor manage their finances and build assets. Insurance services can protect the poor against risks such as illness, accident, and natural disasters.

The design process also involves determining the delivery mechanism. This can be a microfinance institution (MFI), a bank, or a non-governmental organization (NGO). MFIs are specialized institutions that provide financial services to the poor. Banks can also provide financial services to the poor, but they often have higher transaction costs and may not be willing to serve the poor due to perceived risks. NGOs can provide financial services as part of their broader development work.

##### Implementation

The implementation of a microfinance program involves setting up the necessary infrastructure, recruiting and training staff, and launching the program. This can be a complex and challenging process, particularly in developing countries where there may be limited financial infrastructure and human resources.

The first step in implementation is to set up the necessary infrastructure. This can include opening bank accounts, establishing loan and savings products, and setting up a system for managing client data. The infrastructure should be designed to be scalable and sustainable, to ensure that the program can continue operating in the long term.

The next step is to recruit and train staff. This can be a critical factor in the success of the program, as the staff will be responsible for delivering the financial services to the clients. The staff should be selected based on their skills, experience, and commitment to the program. They should also receive comprehensive training to ensure that they are equipped to perform their roles effectively.

The final step is to launch the program. This involves promoting the program to the target population, enrolling clients, and providing them with the necessary financial services. The launch should be accompanied by a communication campaign to raise awareness about the program and encourage participation.

In conclusion, the design and implementation of microfinance programs are crucial to their success. They require careful planning and execution, and should be tailored to the specific needs and circumstances of the target population. With the right design and implementation, microfinance can be a powerful tool for improving the financial inclusion and well-being of the poor in developing countries.
