#### 2.2a Climate Change and Politics

Climate change has become a major political issue in recent years, with many countries and organizations taking action to address it. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is a prime example of international cooperation in addressing climate change. The convention, adopted in 1992, sets the overall framework for international cooperation on climate change and is ratified by 197 countries.

The Kyoto Protocol, a legally binding agreement under the UNFCCC, is another example of international cooperation in addressing climate change. It sets targets for industrialized countries to reduce their greenhouse gas emissions and provides a framework for developing countries to participate in efforts to reduce emissions. The protocol has been ratified by 192 countries, but the United States, one of the largest emitters of greenhouse gases, has not ratified it.

The European Union has also taken significant steps to address climate change. In 2008, the EU adopted the Climate and Energy Package, which sets a target of reducing greenhouse gas emissions by 20% by 2020. The package also includes measures to increase the use of renewable energy sources and improve energy efficiency.

In the United States, the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) has taken action to regulate greenhouse gas emissions under the Clean Power Plan. This plan aims to reduce emissions from power plants, which are the largest source of greenhouse gas emissions in the US. However, the plan has faced legal challenges and has not yet been fully implemented.

These examples demonstrate the complex political connections of climate change. The global system is shaped by the actions and policies of individual countries, and climate change has become a major factor in international relations. As the effects of climate change continue to worsen, it is crucial for the global system to work together to address this issue.

 3.1: Climate Change and International Law

Climate change is a global issue that requires a coordinated international response. #### Historical Context

The issue of climate change has been a topic of international concern for decades. In 1988, the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) was established by the United Nations to assess the science related to climate change. In 1992, the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) was adopted, setting the stage for international cooperation on climate change.

The UNFCCC is a legally binding agreement that aims to stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous human interference with the climate system. It also recognizes the need for developed countries to take the lead in reducing emissions and providing financial and technological support to developing countries.

#### Current State of International Climate Politics

Since the adoption of the UNFCCC, there have been several international agreements and initiatives aimed at addressing climate change. In 2015, the Paris Agreement was adopted, setting a goal of limiting global warming to well below 2 degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels. This agreement also includes provisions for countries to increase their commitments over time and for developed countries to provide financial and technological support to developing countries.

However, despite these efforts, the current state of international climate politics remains complex and challenging. Many countries, particularly developing countries, argue that the current agreements do not go far enough to address the impacts of climate change. Additionally, there are ongoing debates about the role of different countries and regions in addressing climate change, as well as the potential impacts of climate change on international relations.

#### Intersection of Climate Change and Other Global Issues

Climate change does not exist in a vacuum; it intersects with other global issues such as security, trade, and development. For example, the impacts of climate change, such as extreme weather events and food insecurity, can exacerbate existing conflicts and create new security threats. Additionally, the transition to a low-carbon economy can have significant implications for trade and economic development.

Furthermore, the potential impacts of climate change on international relations are a cause for concern. As countries grapple with the effects of climate change, there is a risk of increased competition and conflicts over resources and migration. However, there is also potential for cooperation and collaboration in addressing climate change, particularly in areas such as renewable energy and adaptation strategies.

#### Future of International Politics and Climate Change

The future of international politics and climate change is uncertain, but it is clear that continued international cooperation is crucial for addressing this pressing issue. As the impacts of climate change become more severe, there is a growing need for countries to work together to reduce emissions and adapt to the changing climate.

In addition to international agreements and initiatives, there is also a growing movement for climate justice, which recognizes the disproportionate impacts of climate change on marginalized communities and calls for a more equitable and just response to climate change. This movement is gaining traction and could play a significant role in shaping the future of international climate politics.

In conclusion, international law plays a crucial role in addressing climate change, but there are still many challenges and uncertainties in the current state of international climate politics. As we continue to grapple with the impacts of climate change, it is essential to work towards a more comprehensive and equitable response that takes into account the complex intersections of climate change and other global issues.

However, the Montreal Protocol also faced challenges and limitations. One of the main challenges was the unequal distribution of ozone-depleting substances among countries. This led to concerns about the potential for a "race to the bottom" as developing countries continued to produce and consume these substances.

#### 4.3b Climate Change Policies

Climate change policies can be broadly categorized into two types: mitigation policies and adaptation policies. Mitigation policies aim to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and slow down the rate of climate change, while adaptation policies focus on preparing for and responding to the impacts of climate change.

One of the most well-known mitigation policies is the carbon tax, which was proposed by the Clinton administration in the 1990s. The British Thermal Unit (BTU) Tax, as it was called, aimed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions by taxing producers of fossil fuels based on their heat content. This policy was seen as a way to incentivize the use of cleaner and more sustainable energy sources.

Another mitigation policy that has gained traction in recent years is a carbon cap-and-trade system. This policy sets a limit on the total amount of emissions allowed and allows companies to buy and sell emission permits. This creates an incentive for companies to reduce their emissions in order to save on permit costs.

Adaptation policies, on the other hand, focus on preparing for and responding to the impacts of climate change. This includes building infrastructure to withstand extreme weather events, implementing disaster risk reduction strategies, and providing support for communities affected by climate change.

While these policies have been implemented in various forms around the world, their effectiveness in addressing climate change is still a topic of debate. Some argue that these policies are not stringent enough and do not go far enough in reducing emissions, while others argue that they are necessary steps towards a more sustainable future.

In addition to these policies, international organizations also play a crucial role in facilitating global efforts to address climate change. The ultimate objective of the UNFCCC is to achieve stabilization of greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous human interference with the climate system. This is achieved through the implementation of mitigation measures, which aim to reduce greenhouse gas emissions, and adaptation measures, which aim to help countries and communities adapt to the impacts of climate change.

The UNFCCC also establishes the Conference of the Parties (COP), which is the supreme body of the Convention. The COP meets annually to review progress and make decisions on the implementation of the Convention. The COP also adopts decisions and guidelines for the operation of the Convention, including the Kyoto Protocol and the Paris Agreement.

Climate change is a complex issue that has far-reaching impacts on the global system. As the Earth's climate continues to warm, it is altering the delicate balance of the global system, with potential consequences for ecosystems, human societies, and the economy.

#### Climate Change and Ecosystems

Climate change is having a profound impact on ecosystems around the world. As temperatures rise, many species are struggling to adapt to the changing conditions. This is particularly true for species that are already at the edge of their habitat range, as they may not be able to migrate to cooler areas due to physical barriers such as mountains or human settlements.

Invasive species, which are non-native species that can cause harm to the environment, economy, or human health, are also benefiting from climate change. Warmer temperatures are allowing these species to expand their range and establish populations in new areas. This can have significant impacts on native species, as invasive species can outcompete them for resources and disrupt the balance of the ecosystem.

Climate change is also having significant impacts on human societies. As temperatures rise, more frequent and severe heatwaves, droughts, and floods are expected, which can have devastating consequences for human health, food security, and infrastructure. Low-income communities and communities of color are often the most vulnerable to the impacts of climate change, as they may lack the resources to adapt to changing conditions. This is known as climate justice, and it is a crucial aspect of addressing climate change.

#### Climate Change and the Economy

The impacts of climate change on the economy are significant and far-reaching. As temperatures rise, the cost of climate-related disasters, such as hurricanes and wildfires, is expected to increase. This can have a significant impact on the economy, as these disasters can cause billions of dollars in damage and disrupt trade and commerce.

Climate change is also expected to have significant impacts on the energy sector. They also facilitate the sharing of information and resources, and help to coordinate efforts between different countries.

One of the most prominent international organizations working on climate change is the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). This convention, adopted in 1992, sets the overall framework for international cooperation on climate change. It aims to stabilize greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere at a level that would prevent dangerous human interference with the climate system. The UNFCCC also provides a platform for countries to develop and implement their own climate change policies and actions.

Another important international organization working on climate change is the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC). This panel, established by the UNFCCC, is responsible for assessing the scientific, technical, and socio-economic information relevant for understanding climate change. The IPCC also provides recommendations on how to mitigate and adapt to the impacts of climate change.

Addressing climate change in the context of international politics presents both challenges and opportunities. One of the main challenges is the unequal distribution of the impacts of climate change. Developed countries, which have historically contributed the most to greenhouse gas emissions, often face less severe impacts of climate change compared to developing countries. This can lead to tensions and conflicts between countries, as well as challenges in developing effective climate change policies.

However, there are also opportunities for addressing climate change in international politics. For example, the Paris Agreement, adopted in 2015, provides a framework for countries to work together to reduce their greenhouse gas emissions and adapt to the impacts of climate change. This agreement also includes provisions for developing countries to receive financial and technological support from developed countries in their efforts to address climate change.

### Subsection: 5.5a Understanding International Law

International law is a crucial aspect of climate politics. It provides a framework for addressing climate change at the global level, and it plays a significant role in shaping national and international climate policies. In this subsection, we will explore the basics of international law, including its sources and principles.

#### Sources of International Law

International law is derived from various sources, including treaties, customary law, and general principles of law. Treaties are agreements between states that are legally binding. They can be bilateral, involving two states, or multilateral, involving multiple states. Treaties can take various forms, such as conventions, protocols, and agreements. For example, the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is a multilateral treaty that sets the overall framework for international cooperation on climate change.

Customary law is a body of law that is derived from the consistent practice of states. It is considered to be binding on all states, even those that have not participated in its creation. Customary law can be established through state practice and opinio juris, which refers to the belief by states that a particular practice is legally binding. For instance, the principle of common but differentiated responsibilities (CBDR) is a customary law that recognizes the different responsibilities of developed and developing states in addressing climate change.

General principles of law are principles that are considered to be applicable to all states. They are derived from natural law and are considered to be part of customary international law. These principles include the principle of good faith, the principle of non-intervention, and the principle of sovereignty. These principles can be applied to climate change issues, such as the principle of good faith in the implementation of climate policies and the principle of sovereignty in the management of natural resources.

#### Principles of International Law

International law is governed by various principles, including the principle of sovereignty, the principle of non-intervention, and the principle of good faith. The principle of sovereignty recognizes the right of states to govern themselves without external interference. This principle is particularly relevant in the context of climate change, as it allows states to determine their own climate policies and strategies.

The principle of non-intervention prohibits states from interfering in the internal affairs of other states. This principle is relevant in the context of climate change, as it limits the ability of states to intervene in the climate policies of other states. However, this principle can be overridden by the principle of common concern of humankind, which recognizes the global nature of climate change and the need for international cooperation in addressing it.

The principle of good faith requires states to act in accordance with their obligations under international law. This principle is particularly relevant in the context of climate change, as it requires states to implement their climate policies in good faith and to fulfill their obligations under international climate agreements.

In conclusion, understanding international law is crucial for understanding climate politics. It provides a framework for addressing climate change at the global level and shapes national and international climate policies. By understanding the sources and principles of international law, we can better navigate the complexities of climate change and work towards a more sustainable future.





### Subsection: 5.5b Environmental Regimes

Environmental regimes are international agreements and organizations that aim to address environmental issues, including climate change. These regimes provide a framework for cooperation and coordination among states to address common environmental challenges. In this subsection, we will explore the basics of environmental regimes, including their types and key principles.

#### Types of Environmental Regimes

There are several types of environmental regimes, each with its own focus and scope. Some of the most prominent environmental regimes include:

- The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC): This regime is the primary international agreement on climate change. It sets the overall framework for international cooperation on climate change and provides a platform for states to negotiate and implement climate policies.

- The Convention on Biological Diversity (CBD): This regime aims to conserve biodiversity and promote sustainable use of biological resources. It provides a framework for states to work together to protect and restore biodiversity.

- The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species of Wild Fauna and Flora (CITES): This regime regulates international trade in endangered species to prevent overexploitation and promote conservation.

- The International Maritime Organization (IMO): This organization is responsible for regulating international shipping and addressing its environmental impacts. It has implemented several measures to reduce greenhouse gas emissions from shipping.

#### Key Principles of Environmental Regimes

Environmental regimes are guided by several key principles that shape their objectives and operations. These principles include:

- Common but Differentiated Responsibilities (CBDR): This principle recognizes the different responsibilities of developed and developing states in addressing environmental issues. Developed states, which have historically contributed the most to environmental degradation, have a greater responsibility to address these issues.

- Sustainable Development: This principle aims to balance economic, social, and environmental considerations in development policies. It recognizes the interdependence of these three pillars and the need to address them in a holistic manner.

- Precautionary Principle: This principle states that states should take precautionary measures to prevent or minimize potential harm to the environment, even in the absence of full scientific certainty.

- Polluter Pays Principle: This principle holds polluters responsible for the costs of environmental degradation. It aims to incentivize states to reduce their environmental impacts and promote sustainable practices.

- Public Participation: This principle recognizes the importance of public involvement in environmental decision-making. It aims to ensure that the interests of all stakeholders, including local communities, are taken into account in environmental policies.
